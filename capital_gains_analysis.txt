KEY TAX ISSUES TO ADDRESS:

1. PRIMARY RESIDENCE EXCLUSION (Section 121)
   ────────────────────────────────────────
   ✓ GOOD NEWS: You meet the basic 2 out of 5 year requirement (6 out of 7 years)
   
   ✗ CRITICAL ISSUE: "Nonqualified Use" Limitation
   
   The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (2017) introduced complex allocation rules:
   - If property was used as investment property (rental) and then converted
     to primary residence, the gain must be ALLOCATED between periods
   - Gain is prorated based on the ratio of nonqualified use to total holding period
   - In your case: 1 year investment use / 7 years total = 1/7 of gain is NOT excludable
   
   CALCULATION:
   - Total Gain: $900,000
   - Nonqualified Use Period: 1 year (first year as investment)
   - Qualified Use Period: 6 years (as primary residence)
   - Nonqualified Portion of Gain: $900,000 × (1/7) ≈ $128,571
   - Excludable Gain: $900,000 - $128,571 = $771,429
   - Available Section 121 Exclusion: $500,000 (married) or $250,000 (single)
   
   TAXABLE CAPITAL GAIN: 
   If married: $771,429 - $500,000 = $271,429 taxable
   If single: $771,429 - $250,000 = $521,429 taxable

2. DEPRECIATION RECAPTURE (CRITICAL!)
   ──────────────────────────────────
   ✗ MAJOR TAX ISSUE: This may be your biggest tax bill
   
   - Any depreciation claimed while property was investment rental is taxed
     at ordinary income rates (25%) even though capital gain rates apply
   - You did NOT mention depreciation, but if you claimed it or your tax
     preparer claimed it for the investment years, this is significant
   - This recapture is NOT covered by the Section 121 exclusion
   
   EXAMPLE: If you claimed $100,000 depreciation during rental years:
   - Depreciation Recapture Tax: $100,000 × 25% = $25,000
   - This is in ADDITION to capital gains tax

3. INVESTMENT IN NEW HOME ($700,000 Atlanta home)
   ───────────────────────────────────────────────
   ✗ DOES NOT HELP with capital gains tax
   
   KEY POINT: Purchasing a new home does NOT offset or reduce the capital
   gains tax liability from selling the investment property.
   
   Why: The Section 121 exclusion and capital gains taxes are completely
   separate from subsequent home purchases. The new home only creates a new
   basis for future appreciation tracking.
   
   The home purchase might affect your:
   - Mortgage interest deduction (capped at $750K loan amount)
   - Property tax deduction (subject to SALT limits)
   - But these are different from capital gains on the sold property

WHAT DOES NOT HELP (Common Misconceptions):

1. ✗ Buying a new $700,000 home: Does NOT reduce capital gains tax
   - Future appreciation on new home is separate issue
   - No carryover or offset of gains from old property

2. ✗ 1031 Exchange: NOT AVAILABLE
   - 1031 Exchanges only work for investment property to investment property
   - Cannot use 1031 to exchange investment property for primary residence
   - Once you lived in it as primary residence for significant period,
     conversion back to investment use then exchange is complex and risky

3. ✗ Using proceeds to pay down Atlanta home mortgage: No tax benefit
   - Capital gains tax is owed regardless of what you do with proceeds
   - Paying down mortgage doesn't reduce the gain

4. ✗ Reinvesting proceeds in stocks or other investments: No tax benefit
   - Deferral taxes only work for 1031 exchanges (investment property only)
   - Stock purchases don't provide any offset
